Specifications and Guidelines to make business-related datasets interoperable

Goal statement

These guidelines target data owners, data publishers and data re-users. They aim at two main goals.

  1. The first goal is to allow data re-users to work conveniently with data about businesses published by various data publishers.
  2. The second goal is to enable data re-users to prepare their own data to be easily used together with existing business-related data published according to the STIRData specification.

Data owners and data publishers

There are several reasons why making data easy to access and use will benefit the data owner/publisher.

The main motivation is that it will often be important to achieve the mandate of the organisation; having a national Business Register contributes to the economy through a more well-functioning market. This is achieved by establishing an important and authoritative source of information about the actors in the market, thereby reducing risk and transaction costs for those who are interested in doing business with them. By making the information more accessible and usable, and supporting cross-border use, increases the effect.

In addition, there is also the benefit that increased use of data increases the data quality; errors are discovered and addressed. Finally, by publishing data in a way that makes it easy for the users, will reduce time/effort spent in responding to questions about how to use and interpret data.

The Directive (EU) 2019/1024 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 20 June 2019 on open data and the re-use of public sector information introduced so-called high-value datasets, which, when used repeatedly by various data processors, will have interesting benefits for the European economy and society. The list of those datasets is currently under final approval. Following approval, the listed datasets will have to be published in all EU member states as open data, i.e. in a machine-readable and open format, without restricting terms of use, available for free on the Internet.

The concept of high-value datasets pertains to referred thematic categories, which currently are as follows:

  • Geospatial
  • Earth observation and environment
  • Meteorological
  • Statistics
  • Companies and company ownership
  • Mobility

The implementing act (draft Regulation under final approval) fulfils the goals of the directive 2019/1024/EU and the entire concept of high-value datasets by dictating not just the datasets which the member states ought to provide but also the minimal requirements on data quality properties of the published data so that the quality of the data with regards to the aforementioned categories is guaranteed.

Thanks to the Draft Regulation, EU member states will take the first step towards creating a unified EU open data space, in which data processors can create services that are usable EU-wide. That would be interesting progress compared to the current state, where the creation of similar services is very difficult. It is often the case that a dataset provided in one EU state is not available at all in other states or is not available as open data (e.g. access is subject to fees). While the creation of a unified space can be done in other areas, it is necessary to complete many steps in order to unify the open data space.

Company, insolvency and beneficial ownership datasets are unanimously considered of high value by the literature and the reusers and many use cases have already been mapped for this type of information. The EU Business Graph project for instance created a categorization of business use cases which is useful to help conceptualise the re-use of company data by private stakeholders and NGOs, defining three main sectors: the business information sector, the marketing and sales sector, and the business publishing sector.

Most of the value and benefits linked to the reuse of company and company ownership information are concentrated in the economic and reuse categories, although the social value is also very high and links to their potential for crime and fraud detection. When combined with procurement data in particular, company information also helps understanding governments’ expenditure and increase citizens' trust and governments’ accountability.

Standardisation at different levels (such as metadata schemata, data representation formats and licensing conditions of open data) is essential to enable broad data integration, data exchange and interoperability with the overall goal of fostering innovation based on data. Overall, because of the mandatory publishing of high value open company and registry datasets due to the Regulation to be adopted, the application of standard and shared formats and protocols for gathering and processing data from different sources in a coherent and interoperable manner across sectors and vertical markets is what StirData offers in a robust and concrete way.

Data re-users

The main motivation is to reduce time/effort spent from when relevant data are discovered until they deliver value for the business, in terms of for instance automatisation or analytics/insights.

Examples of time-consuming efforts from discovery until value, are:

  • Finding the data: Which data exists that are relevant for my business?
  • Accessing the data; finding the authoritative source of the data, how to access it, and being authorised for access
  • Evaluating the data: Quality assurance to avoid misinterpretations and wrong conclusions, that might result from mistaking seemingly identical information as representing the same – same term, different meaning – or vice versa – different term, same meaning
  • “Data wrangling”; trying to fit data of low quality, with limited consistency and documentation to internal data structures

The above examples all have in common that they might be hard on a national level, but becomes even harder as soon as data crosses the borders, and the business must deal with different languages, different information structures, different national structures and responsible authorities, different legislation and pricing models for accessing data etc etc.

Identification of business-related datasets

In these guidelines, we distinguish two kinds of business-related datasets.

The first kind we call a “registry-like” dataset. This may be an actual business registry, or a similar dataset where the focal point of the dataset is a business. The dataset is typically structured as a list of businesses and some data about them. For these kinds of datasets, we will aim at compliance with the STIRData specification.

The second kind of business-related datasets is typically focused on some domain-specific topic and contains links to related businesses. Examples of such datasets may be lists of public contracts, reports about check actions, sources of pollution, tourist attractions, etc. With this kind of datasets, we will aim at their representation in an interoperable data format based on the RDF data model, and proper linking, or preparation for it, to a related “registry-like” dataset.

In the goal statement we mentioned that company related data have been identified as having high socio-economical value, and the “registry-like” data will be mandatorily published as open data throughout the EU. The STIRData specification could serve as the technical guideline on how to publish those datasets.

The company-related datasets can be further combined with other datasets, e.g. characterising regions in the Linked Data fashion. Those are, however, out of scope of this document.

In any case, for us to be able to work with the data, we need to gain technical access to it and, if possible, a contact point to help with the transformation, or adopt it. Technical access to the data may be in the form of access to an API, or in the form of a data dump, which may be updated regularly.

Analysis of current business-related datasets

Once we have technical access to the data we want to make interoperable, we need to make sure that we understand the data correctly. This understanding may be based either on a pre-existing documentation of the dataset, or an analysis of the contents of the dataset.

In the case of datasets originating in the public administration, such as business registries, there is typically national or regional legislation mandating the creation and maintenance of the dataset, including exact definitions of what is to be represented in the data.

The goal of the dataset analysis is to answer the following questions:

  1. Which concepts are represented in the data?
  • Example of an answer would be: Company, its name, identification number and registered address
  1. What are the definitions of the concepts?
  • The definitions typically originate in national legislation. Or, the definition may correspond to one in an existing vocabulary such as the EU Core Business Vocabulary. For example, in this vocabulary, the core concept is a Legal Entity, with the following definition: “A self-employed person, company, or organisation that has legal rights and obligations.”
  1. What are the codelists used in the dataset? For example,
  • NUTS codes to represent the location of an address
  • Statistical Classification of Economic Activities in the European Community (NACE Rev. 2) codes to represent activities of a business
  • Company Type, or Entity Legal Form to further specify the kind of the business
  • National versions of the European codelists, e.g. CZ-NACE, etc.
  • Other codelists?
  1. How are the codelists published and used in the dataset? For example,
  • NACE Rev. 2
  1. published as part of EU Vocabularies
  2. in RDF/XML as a dump
  3. IRI dereference
  4. Dataset contains e.g.
  1. NACE codes
  2. NACE IRIs
  3. Activities that we know how to map to NACE codes/IRIs
  4. No information based on which a link to NACE could be established
  • NUTS 2021
  1. published as part of EU Vocabularies
  2. in RDF/XML as a dump
  3. IRI dereference
  4. Dataset contains e.g.
  1. NUTS codes
  2. NUTS IRIs
  3. Addresses that we know how to map to NUTS codes/IRIs
  4. No information based on which a link to NUTS could be established
  • Entity Legal Form
  1. Published as CSV and as Excel file
  2. needs to be transformed to RDF, IRI patterns need to be established

Known issues of current business related datasets

During the STIRData project, we have identified a few common issues which currently hinder cross-border semantic interoperability of business-related datasets.

Business-related data based on national legislation

The term “business” itself, the data collected about businesses, and the terms describing various types of businesses are all defined by national legislation, with no EU legislation acting as a single source of truth. For example, the term “business” in one EU country comprises physical people doing business, while in another EU country it does not.

Another example is with business types. What is considered to be a “limited liability company” in one EU country may be quite different from what is considered to be a “limited liability company” in another. This means that it is definitely not enough to, e.g. rely on similarity of English translations of the business type names with the assessment of whether the two company types are the same or not. This is, however, an issue that needs to be resolved on the legislation level, and is out of scope of our project. All we can say is that there is the ISO 20275: Entity Legal Forms Code List standard, which contains all known company types from individual countries and their English names. But even the ISO standard does not aim at inferring relations among company types with similar names in English.

Various definitions of parts of companies

Another issue we came across is the structure of larger companies. In one country, a company is registered as a whole in a business registry, where it has its identifier and a registered address, and there is no information about actual points of service, while in another country, each company point of service has an individual registration in the business registry, possibly each with a different set of business activities. These kinds of differences then make it difficult to consistently work with the data in a cross-border fashion.

Most business registries data include a single entry for a company, but e.g. Belgium and France business registries include different entries for enterprises and legal entities, as they call respectively the main business units, which can have establishments and branches. Moreover, in the case of France a legal entity does not have an address, but it has a principal establishment where the address is registered. Also, the Norwegian business distinguishes between main units and subunits entries, each with its own registered address and business activities, a division that is made by the legislation of the particular country.

National-level codelists

Finally, during data preparation, we also link the company addresses to Local Administrative Unit (LAU) codes which help applications working with the data to aggregate it on a finer level than using the NUTS codes. The LAU codes are, however, not yet published by the EU Publications Office as Linked Data, and therefore we had to do the transformation and publication ourselves. The resulting dataset in data.europa.eu is accessible at https://data.europa.eu/data/datasets/https-lkod-mff-cuni-cz-zdroj-datove-sady-stirdata-lau-stirdata.

Transformation to interoperable format

The goal of the transformation is to make the datasets interoperable with others, e.g. as seen in our architecture, which can be exploited by any data consumer, one example being the STIRData platform.

The data model that ensures technical interoperability of data on the Web is the Resource Description Framework (RDF) and the Linked Data principles. The specification that ensures semantic interoperability of the published dataset is then the STIRData specification.

In this section, we show two examples of datasets transformed from their original format to the one in RDF according to the STIRData specification. One uses LinkedPipes ETL and the other one uses D2RML as the tool for the transformation. However, since all techniques used in the process are based on web standards, there are many other tools that may be used or even developed to facilitate the transformation.

An important part of the transformation of data to RDF besides adhering to the specification of RDF classes and predicates to be used is establishing IRI patterns for the created data entities. For instance, in the Czech dataset, we use the following patterns:

  • Companies will get an IRI that looks like:
    https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/ + national ID of the company, e.g. https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/27463095.
  • The company identifier will get an IRI which consists of the IRI of the company and “/registrace”, e.g.:
    https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/27463095/registrace
  • For addresses, we use the prefix https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/adresa/ and a value of a MD5 hash of the individual address fields, e.g.:
    https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/adresa/ed527c038368896a0e11ec8e0d940d72
  • etc.

Example of data transformation using LinkedPipes ETL

In this section, we show how data from the Czech business registry, already available in RDF according to a national ontology, can be transformed using LinkedPipes ETL to be compliant with the STIRData specification. The prerequisite is the reader knows the RDF data model and its serialisation RDF Turtle, and the SPARQL query language.

First of all, we need to take a look at what data we have on the input, and how the data should look like on the output. Note that we present a subset of the actual data - a company, its name, identifier and type to demonstrate the process itself.

Input, output and transformation query

On the input, the record about a company using the Czech ontology in RDF Turtle looks like this:

@prefix vr: <https://slovník.gov.cz/legislativní/sbírka/304/2013/pojem/> .

@prefix ok: <https://slovník.gov.cz/legislativní/sbírka/90/2012/pojem/> .

@prefix company: <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/> .

company:27463095 a vr:akciová-společnost ;

    ok:firma-obchodní-korporace "TechnoPark Management a.s. v likvidaci"@cs ;

    vr:identifikační-číslo-osoby "27463095" .

We can see the identifier vr:identifikační-číslo-osoby of the company, its name ok:firma-obchodní-korporace and its type as a vr:akciová-společnost saying it is a joint-stock company. Note that to be able to process the data, one needs to understand it properly.

According to the STIRData specification, the same information should look like this:

@prefix skos: <http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#> .

@prefix adms: <http://www.w3.org/ns/adms#> .

@prefix legal: <http://www.w3.org/ns/legal#> .


@prefix company: <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/> .

company:27463095 a legal:LegalEntity;

    legal:legalName "TechnoPark Management a.s. v likvidaci"@cs;

    legal:legalIdentifier <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/27463095/registrace> .

<https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/27463095/registrace>

    a adms:Identifier;

    skos:notation "27463095" .

In order to do the transformation, we prepare a SPARQL CONSTRUCT query to do that matching the input in the WHERE clause and producing the output in the CONSTRUCT clause. The query will look like this:

PREFIX legal: <http://www.w3.org/ns/legal#>

PREFIX adms: <http://www.w3.org/ns/adms#>

PREFIX skos: <http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#>

PREFIX vr: <https://slovník.gov.cz/legislativní/sbírka/304/2013/pojem/>

PREFIX ok: <https://slovník.gov.cz/legislativní/sbírka/90/2012/pojem/>

CONSTRUCT {

  ?company a legal:LegalEntity ;

    legal:legalName ?name ;

    legal:legalIdentifier ?reg .

  ?reg a adms:Identifier ;

    skos:notation ?id .

}

WHERE {

  ?company ok:firma-obchodní-korporace ?name ;

    vr:identifikační-číslo-osoby ?id .

  BIND(IRI(CONCAT(STR(?company), "/registrace")) AS ?reg)

}

Data transformation pipeline

Now, we are ready to create a pipeline in LinkedPipes ETL processing the sample and producing its STIRData compliant equivalent.

In the pipeline, we use the Text holder component to contain the data sample shown above as an RDF Turtle file. Next, we load the data from the file into an in-memory triple store using the Files to RDF single graph component. Next, we execute the SPARQL construct query to produce the STIRData compliant output and finally, we use RDF to file to store the output in an RDF Turtle file we see as the output sample.

When everything is working OK with the data sample, we can change the input to actually contain the entire Czech business registry. No other change is required, only the pipeline will run longer as it transforms the entire dataset.

Here, we used the Files from local component to load the input data file into the pipeline instead of the data sample.

Note that there are tutorials ready for more complex LinkedPipes ETL use cases for transformation from CSV, XML and JSON file formats.

Mapping of postal codes to LAU codes

In this section, we will show how we can link company data containing addresses with postal codes to Local Administrative Units (LAU codes) using LinkedPipes ETL. Note that the LAU codes are currently not published officially as Linked Data, so we need to use our own RDF version of those based on the Eurostat’s Postal Codes dataset.

In this mapping pipeline, we load a postcode to LAU mapping in a CSV file and automatically transform it to RDF using the Tabular component.

The source in CSV looks like below - the relevant columns are POSTCODE and NSI_CODE_2.

X,Y,OBJECTID,POSTCODE,CNTR_ID,PC_CNTR,NUTS3_2021,CODE,GISCO_ID_2,NSI_CODE_2,LAT_NAT,LAU_LATIN,COASTAL,CITY_ID,GREATER_CI,FUA_ID,DGURBA

15.831384298999978,49.42037417099999,32595,588 25,CZ,CZ_588 25,'CZ063','588 25',CZ_587265,587265,Jersin,,NO,,,CZ014L2,3

15.779748760000018,49.367128072000014,32596,588 23,CZ,CZ_588 23,'CZ063','588 23',CZ_587346,587346,Kamenice,,NO,,,CZ014L2,3

The relevant source subset in RDF after Tabular component applied to automatically transform the CSV file to RDF:

_:node1gfljvj3ex5692 <urn:POSTCODE> "588 25";

  <urn:NSI_CODE_2> "587265" .

On the input in the other branch, we load the Czech business registry according to the STIRData specification, which includes a company’s registered address, specifically, a postal code, according to the STIRData specification and the Core Location Vocabulary. We load this file into a triplestore so that we can transform it using SPARQL.

@prefix locn: <http://www.w3.org/ns/locn#> .

@prefix legal: <http://www.w3.org/ns/legal#> .

@prefix company: <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/> .

@prefix addresses: <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/adresa/> .

company:27463095 a legal:LegalEntity;

    legal:registeredAddress addresses:7a1492f2c7ed474e09ad8d38c8f0bac6 .

addresses:7a1492f2c7ed474e09ad8d38c8f0bac6 a locn:Address ;

    locn:postCode "53006".

The expected output should look like:

@prefix m8g: <http://data.europa.eu/m8g/> .

@prefix stirdata: <https://w3id.org/stirdata/vocabulary/> .

 

@prefix company: <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/> .

@prefix addresses: <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/adresa/> .

@prefix lau: <https://w3id.org/stirdata/resource/lau/item/> .

@prefix adminUnitLevel: <https://w3id.org/stirdata/resource/adminUnitLevel/> .

 

addresses:7a1492f2c7ed474e09ad8d38c8f0bac6

    m8g:adminUnit lau:CZ_587265 ;

    stirdata:lau lau:CZ_587265 .

lau:CZ_587265 a m8g:AdminUnit;

    m8g:level adminUnitLevel:LAU ;

    m8g:code lau:CZ_587265 .

Therefore, the SPARQL CONSTRUCT query doing the transformation then looks like:

PREFIX locn: <http://www.w3.org/ns/locn#>

PREFIX m8g: <http://data.europa.eu/m8g/>

PREFIX stirdata: <https://w3id.org/stirdata/vocabulary/>

PREFIX adminUnitLevel: <https://w3id.org/stirdata/resource/adminUnitLevel/>

CONSTRUCT {

  ?a stirdata:lau ?lau ;

    m8g:adminUnit ?lau .

  ?lau a m8g:AdminUnit ;

    m8g:code ?lau ;

    m8g:level adminUnitLevel:LAU .

} WHERE {

  [] <urn:POSTCODE> ?pc ;

     <urn:NSI_CODE_2> ?lauCode .

 

  BIND(REPLACE(?pc, " ","") AS ?normalizedPostCode)

  ?a a locn:Address;

     locn:postCode ?normalizedPostCode .

 

  BIND(IRI(CONCAT("https://w3id.org/stirdata/resource/lau/item/CZ_", ?lauCode)) AS ?lau)

}

The result is a linkset linking addresses to LAU codes. The data can be loaded into a SPARQL endpoint together with the business registry data, and saved in an RDF file for download, similarly to other data.

Mapping from a national NACE extensions

Often enough, individual countries extend the Statistical Classification of Economic Activities in the European Community Rev. 2 (NACE Rev. 2) code list with national specifics and they use this national extension in their business registry.

For a NACE national extension item to be usable, it needs to be described sufficiently. Items corresponding to NACE level 1 through 4 need to be linked using skos:exactMatch to the central NACE code list. Example:

<https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz/položky/6820> a stirdata:BusinessActivity, skos:Concept ;

    skos:prefLabel "Pronájem a správa vlastních nebo pronajatých nemovitostí"@cs ;

    skos:notation "6820" ;

    skos:inScheme <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz> ;

    stirdata:level 4 ;

    skos:broader <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz/položky/682> ;

    skos:exactMatch <http://data.europa.eu/ux2/nace2/6820> .

Note that in this example, NACE level 4 item 6820 is mapped by skos:exactMatch to http://data.europa.eu/ux2/nace2/6820.

Items of lower levels need to be mapped to items of higher levels using skos:broader. Example:

<https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz/položky/27900> a stirdata:BusinessActivity, skos:Concept ;

    skos:prefLabel "Výroba ostatních elektrických zařízení"@cs ;

    skos:notation "27900" ;

    skos:inScheme <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz> ;

    stirdata:level 5 ;

    skos:broader <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz/položky/2790> .

<https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz/položky/2790> a stirdata:BusinessActivity, skos:Concept ;

    skos:prefLabel "Výroba ostatních elektrických zařízení"@cs ;

    skos:notation "2790" ;

    skos:inScheme <https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/číselníky/nace-cz> ;

    stirdata:level 4 ;

    skos:exactMatch <http://data.europa.eu/ux2/nace2/2790> .

Example of data transformation using D2RML

In this section, we describe how the data from the Norwegian business registry, available in JSON format by Brønnøysund Register Centre, can be transformed to be compliant with the STIRData specification using D2RML.

The latest entire Norwegian business registry data are provided in JSON format by the API call https://data.brreg.no/enhetsregisteret/api/enheter/lastned. The call returns the gzipped file called enheter_alle.json.gz, and enheter_alle.json is a long JSON array containing one object entry for each company. A shortened example of such an entry is the following:

{

  "organisasjonsnummer" : "810034882",

  "navn" : "SANDNES ELEKTRISKE FORRETNING AS",

  "organisasjonsform" : {

    "kode" : "AS",

    "beskrivelse" : "Aksjeselskap",

  },

  "registreringsdatoEnhetsregisteret" : "1995-02-19",

  "naeringskode1" : {

    "beskrivelse" : "Elektrisk installasjonsarbeid",

    "kode" : "43.210"

  },

  "naeringskode2" : {

    "beskrivelse" : "Butikkhandel med belysningsutstyr",

    "kode" : "47.592"

  },

  "forretningsadresse" : {

    "land" : "Norge",

    "landkode" : "NO",

    "postnummer" : "4306",

    "poststed" : "SANDNES",

    "adresse" : [ "Langgata 87" ],

    "kommune" : "SANDNES",

    "kommunenummer" : "1108"

  },

  "stiftelsesdato" : "1977-06-15",

}

The above entry includes fields for the company number, company name, company type, company activity codes, address and foundation and registration dates. A possible output for this entry according to the STIRData specification looks like this:

@prefix adms: <http://www.w3.org/ns/adms#> .

@prefix dct: <http://purl.org/dc/terms/> .

@prefix legal: <http://www.w3.org/ns/legal#> .

@prefix locn: <http://www.w3.org/ns/locn#> .

@prefix m8g: <http://data.europa.eu/m8g/> .

@prefix nuts: <http://data.europa.eu/nuts/code/> .

@prefix schema: <https://schema.org/> .

@prefix skos: <http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#> .

@prefix xsd: <http://www.w3.org/2001/XMLSchema#> .

The last missing part is the generation of the following triples:

lau:NO_1102 a m8g:AdminUnit;

    m8g:level adminUnitLevel:LAU ;

    m8g:code lau:NO_1102 .

    m8g:level adminUnitLevel:NUTS-3 ;

    m8g:code lau:NO0A1 .

The particularity of these triples with respect to the ones generated by the mapping presented so far is that they are not specific to a company but are necessary according to the STIRData model as they provide qualitative characterizations for the uris that are used as objects of the m8g:adminUnit predicate in the companies' addresses. This means that producing these triples from the data of each company will lead to generation of many duplicate triples. For this reason, the D2RML approach is to first generate a temporary in-memory RDF graph where to write the generated triples from each company's data and then export the triples from that model to the final output. Since the RDF graph cannot contain duplicate triples, this process will eliminate the duplicates and produce the desired result.

To achieve the above we have to define first the temporary model that will hold in memory an RDF graph

<#AdminLevelModel>

        a dris:AuxiliaryModel .

and then add to the original mapping the following triples maps that generate the desired triples. Note that these maps instead of the standard output they use the above in-memory model.

<#EnheterMapping> 

   ...

      dr:logicalOutput <#AdminLevelModel> ;

      rr:subjectMap [

         rr:template "{@nuts}{PostCodeTransformation~~$.NUTS3}";

         rr:class adminUnitlevel:NUTS-3 ;

      ] ;

   ] ;

      rr:subjectMap [

         rr:template "{@lau}{PostCodeTransformation~~$.LAU}";

      ] ;

   ] .

The above will add to the temporary model the triples lau:NO_1102 a adminUnitLevel:NUTS-3 and  nuts:NO0A1 a adminUnitLevel:LAU.          rr:column "uri";

         rr:class m8g:AdminUnit ;

      ] ;

         rr:predicate  m8g:code ;

         rr:objectMap  [

            rr:column "uri" ;

            rr:termType  rr:IRI ;

         ]

      ] ;

            rr:termType rr:IRI ;

         ]

      ] ;  

   ] .

Presenting and publishing data in RDF

Once the data is ready in the interoperable format, in RDF, as Linked Data, and, in the case of business-registry-like datasets, according to the STIRData specification, it is time to decide how that data will be presented for internal use, or published for public use. Publishing the data as RDF dump is a must, IRI dereference is recommended, and running a SPARQL endpoint is required for applications such as the STIRData platform.

SPARQL endpoint

In the context of the STIRData platform and business-registry-like datasets, it is required that the data is available through a publicly accessible SPARQL endpoint, which allows applications like the STIRData platform to query the data without needing to copy all of it or dereference data items one by one. However, in general, running a SPARQL endpoint requires resources at the data publisher’s side to run and maintain the endpoint.

RDF dump

A complementary way of publishing data in RDF is through RDF dumps in various RDF data formats such as RDF Turtle or JSON-LD. This way of publishing the data requires minimal resources at the publisher’s side, but every data consumer needs to download the entire dataset to be able to work with it.

IRI dereference

The third standard way of publishing Linked Data is through IRI dereference. In this way, when a user accesses an IRI of a thing like a business, e.g. https://obchodní-rejstřík.stirdata.opendata.cz/zdroj/registrované-osoby/27463095, they get an RDF representation of the given business. This is ideal to get data about a specific thing, however, it becomes impractical for accessing larger subsets of the datasets.

IRI dereference can be easily implemented on top of a SPARQL endpoint containing the data, which might not even be publicly accessible for generic SPARQL querying, if performance and maintenance costs would be unacceptable.

Other approaches: #LD, LDES

Besides IRI dereference, RDF dumps and SPARQL endpoints, there are also other approaches to publishing linked data emerging. However, they are not mature and widely adopted yet, so we mention them for future reference.

The first approach is Linked Data Fragments (#LD), restricting the querying capabilities of the endpoint to lists of simple triple pattern fragments, leaving the computational complexity of joining those fragments to answer an actual query to the client. The triple pattern fragments can be cached using standard web cache technologies, further distributing the effort needed to query the dataset.

The second approach is Linked Data Event Streams (LDES), focusing on allowing consumers to easily replicate the source dataset and keep in sync with its latest changes with minimum effort required from the data publisher.

The key is to provide the dcterms:conformsTo <https://stirdata.github.io/data-specification/> link on the dataset, and to have a distribution of that dataset representing a SPARQL Endpoint data service, indicated by dcterms:conformsTo <https://www.w3.org/TR/sparql11-protocol/> on the dcat:DataService.

Typically, the dataset is registered in an organisational, regional or national DCAT-AP compliant open data catalogue from where it gets harvested to the Official portal for European data.

The SPARQL query that can be used by applications searching for compliant datasets in DCAT-AP data catalogues looks like this:

PREFIX dcterms: <http://purl.org/dc/terms/>

PREFIX dcat: <http://www.w3.org/ns/dcat#>

PREFIX rdf: <http://www.w3.org/1999/02/22-rdf-syntax-ns#>

PREFIX rdfs: <http://www.w3.org/2000/01/rdf-schema#>

SELECT ?title ?endpoint WHERE {

  ?dataset a dcat:Dataset ;

    dcterms:title ?title ;

    dcterms:conformsTo <https://stirdata.github.io/data-specification/> ;

    dcat:distribution/dcat:accessService ?service .

  ?service a dcat:DataService ;

    dcterms:conformsTo <https://www.w3.org/TR/sparql11-protocol/> ;

    dcat:endpointURL ?endpoint .

  FILTER(langMatches(LANG(?title), "en"))

}

It can be tried out on the SPARQL endpoint of the Official portal for European data using Yasgui.

LP-ETL profiling pipeline

For providers and consumers of business registry data compliant with the STIRData specification there is a LinkedPipes ETL profiling pipeline ready.

It searches the Official portal for European data for datasets compliant with the STIRData specification via the dcterms:conformsTo link in their metadata. Optionally, another DCAT-AP compliant data catalogue accessible through a SPARQL endpoint can be provided. Specifically, the SPARQL endpoint distributions of those datasets are found. This way, compliant datasets properly recorded in the data catalogue are found.

In the next step, queries profiling the data in the found SPARQL endpoints according to the specification are generated and executed, returning statistics about numbers of entities found with various items from the STIRData specification available.

In the final step, samples of IRIs of entities with missing select items from the specification are identified in the found SPARQL endpoints and added to the statistics.

Legal interoperability

The following guideline summarises the legal analysis presented in the legal report in a form of a checklist. To make further use of the data at European level a success, the re-user must have a high level of legal certainty that there are not any legal obstacles for further use of the data. This issue, however, must be properly addressed by the data providers. The following checklist should serve as a tool that helps data providers to be sure that they have not neglected any important issue during the process of data publication.

Step 1: Access to the data

  1. Check regulation of access of information
  • What laws do govern access and publication of information?
  • How was the Open Data Directive implemented into the national legal order?
  • Is there any specific act that covers publication of datasets of public registers of companies and other legal entities?
  • If yes, this act will generally be applicable.
  • If not,a general freedom of access to information or similar act will generally be applicable.
  1. Does the public sector body have a legal duty to publish the data?
  • Are the data part of HVD in accordance with OD Directive?
  • Is there a national regulation that constitutes the duty to publish the data?
  • How precisely is the duty stipulated? (E.g. is it general like “Data from the register”, or is it a more specific enumeration of data categories?)
  1. Does the public sector body have a discretion to publish data?
  • Can the public sector body generally publish data without a specific legal obligation to do so?
  • This will apply in cases, when there is not a duty to publish specific datasets, or in cases, where there is such a duty, but the public sector body would like to publish more than the duty prescribes.

Step 2: Intellectual property rights protection

  1. For a proper licensing scheme it is necessary to identify protected content and make sure that the data provider can licence it.
  2. Each dataset can be subject to several intellectual property rights.
  3. Are plain data in your jurisdiction protected by any kind of intellectual property or property rights?
  • If no (which will probably be the most cases), this will not constitute an obstacle for publication and re-use of data
  • If yes, it is important to identify, whether the data provider is owner of the data (or whether they exercise the rights to the content)
  • If yes, the content can be provided and licensed under public licence (preferably CC0 or CC BY 4.0).
  • If not, the content cannot be provided.
  1. Does the dataset contain copyrighted content?
  2. E.g. there are copyrighted literal or artistic works in the dataset, or the dataset is a map
  • If no (which will probably be the most cases), this will not constitute an obstacle for publication and re-use of data
  • If yes, it is important to identify, whether the data provider can exercise the rights to the content
  • If yes, the content can be provided and licensed under public licence (preferably CC BY 4.0, or CC0 if your jurisdiction allows to waive the copyright).
  • If not, the content cannot be provided.
  1. Is the database protected by copyright itself?
  2. I.e., are there fulfilled the requirements that a) it is the author's intellectual creation, and b) the selection of the elements and their arrangement must be the result of creative activity?
  • If no (which will probably be the most cases), this will not constitute an obstacle for publication and re-use of data
  • If yes, it is important to identify, whether the data provider can exercise the rights to the content (which will probably be most of the cases)
  • In some cases, it is possible to provide a dataset without licensing the copyrighted database. It will depend on whether the export of the database directly follows its structure and selection of elements. If not, then the exported dataset will not infringe the copyright.
  • If yes, the database can be provided and licensed under public licence (preferably CC BY 4.0, or CC0 if your jurisdiction allows to waive the copyright).
  • If not, the content cannot be provided.
  1. Is the database protected by sui generis right?
  2. I.e. there has been qualitatively and/or quantitatively a substantial investment in either the obtaining, verification or presentation of the contents
  3. This step is essential because sui generis database rights directly affect the provided content.
  • If no, this will not constitute an obstacle for publication and re-use of data
  • If yes, it is important to identify, whether the data provider is maker of the database (which will be most of the cases) or whether they can exercise the rights to the content
  • If yes, the database can be provided and must be licensed for further re-use, preferably under public licence (CC BY 4.0, or CC0 if your jurisdiction allows to waive the sui generis database right).
  • If not, the content cannot be provided.

Step 3: Personal data protection

  1. If the dataset contains personal data, it is essential that they are provided only in accordance with personal data protection law (primarily GDPR) and that the data re-users are properly informed about the presence of personal data, so they can effectively comply with their duties as data controllers.
  2. Does the dataset contain personal data?
  • If no, this will not constitute an obstacle for publication and re-use of data
  • If yes, is there a legal duty to provide such data as open data?
  • If yes, the dataset can be provided
  • If not, the content cannot be provided.
  1. The data provider can lower the risk of misuse of the provided personal data by conditioning their provision with a conclusion of a contract, which would bind the re-user to use the data in a certain foreseeable way. E.g. the data provider can limit purposes of re-use. However, it must be noted that because of such further conditions, it would not be possible to identify such provided information as open data.
  2. If the personal data cannot be provided, the data provider still can conduct a thorough anonymisation, which would render personal data non-personal. It is important to note, that the anonymisation has to be prepared thoroughly and diligently because there is a higher risk of de-anonymisation, once the dataset is publicly and freely available.

Step 4: Preparation of Terms of use

  1. Once the data provider identifies legal obstacles that could prevent the publication or re-use of the data, it is essential to communicate results of their findings to the data re-user.
  2. Terms of use should contain information about any content that is subjected to any kind of information protection, whether it is copyright or personal data protection. It also must contain any solution which overcomes such protection, such as licences.
  3. Terms of use should be easily accessible and preferably part of the metadata of the dataset.
  4. For the sake of legal certainty, it is advisable to precisely identify what intellectual property rights are present in the specific instances of the provided dataset and how precisely they are licensed.
  • E.g. the data provider can decide, that copyrighted content is licensed with CC BY 4.0 public licence and at the same time, that sui generis database right is waived with CC0.
  1. If the dataset contains personal data, the terms of use must contain this information. It is not possible to licence personal data. The re-user will have to fulfil all the duties arising from GDPR and national laws of data protection.
  2. If the dataset does not contain any protected content, the terms of use should state this information for the sake of legal certainty.
  3. The data provider can provide the data under other conditions and after a conclusion of a contract. However, imposing of such additional obligations for data re-use would lead to a situation, in which the provided content would no longer be truly open data.
  • Furthermore, should the data provider decide to do so, firstly they must ensure, how the national law allows for and what are conditions for a valid conclusion of a contract online.
